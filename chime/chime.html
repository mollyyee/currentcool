chime

ui ux design | april 2017



We can empower survivors if we can make people listen to them.

In the 24-hour Social Norms Hackathon, I worked with a team of 5 and won 1st place with Chime, a storytelling platform aiming to give survivors of sexual violence a voice.
problem
1. The sexual violence is often not acknowledged by the unaffected general public.
2. Survivors of sexual violence are uninclined to speak up, because their accounts are often belittled.
solution
1. Show the gravity and realness of sexual violence to unaffected bystanders.
2. Create a safe space for survivors to tell their stories, a space where others are compassionately listening.
our insights
1. Statistics, usually used to communicate the impact of various issues, are often distant and impersonal to those who receive them.
2. Survivors who speak up can be viewed as whistleblowers, and their accounts are usually put under heavy scruntiny.
the ideas
1. Anonymously show people how many of their friends are personally affected by sexual violence.
2. Create a platform for people to sift through anonymous accounts of survivor stories.
userflows
After settling on prototyping these two ideas above, we began to flesh the user experience based on our understanding of the user needs.
the solution
Our two-part solution included using a hypothetical Facebook survey to replace impersonal statistics with information of how many of their friends are personally affected by sexual assault, and an app where people could share or listen anonymously to other survivors' voices.
part 1: making it personal
With Sketch, I created the Facebook survey mockups. You can take a look at the Framer prototype here.
First, Facebook would prompt people to take a survey to anonymously find out how they have personally been affected by sexual violence.
considerations
Understanding that this solution deals with an extremely sensitive subject, we embedded designs that would ease users into the topic and allow them to opt out at anytime.
This survey also aimed to inform people about different types of sexual violence. These question bubbles open into definitions.
To make sure that topics do not come up in jarring ways, my team and I spent a lot of time sensitizing the language we used.
Making sure that our users did not receive any information they did not want, we wanted to add predictive indicators showing what they were going to see next in the process.
Now that sexual violence is a personal to the users, we are able to create an audience that is curious and empathetic for the survivors. This will now lead users to our platform, Chime, where they can anonymously hear the survivor voices.
part 2: creating an audience
In the second part of Chime, the user is then taken to an interface that allows them listen to survivor's stories.
Each bubble of light represents a survivor's story. The more survivors submit their anonymous stories, the more light there will be on the interface. Listeners can come onto the platform and click through bubbles, which each reveal small tweet-like snippets of each survivor's stories.
This way, Chime creates a safe and anonymous space to share bits of their experiences.
